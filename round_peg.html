You are a round peg (probably)

There's an (almost) zero chance that you are a clone of your teacher. Your body has its own shape and size. It has its own proportions, strengths and weaknesses. Your mind works differently too. Even identical twins don't move or think in exactly the same way. So don't expect to play the cello in exactly the same way as anyone else. We are all individuals and that is nature's way.

Ever since people started playing the cello they have disagreed about the best method. Different schools of teaching developed different ideas. Even within the same school, great teachers frequently disagreed with one another, yet they all produced wonderful musicians. That should tell us something.

There has never been a single universally accepted way of playing. The techniques may differ, but the principles on which they are built are remarkably similar. These techniques did not appear overnight. They are the result of centuries of experimentation, refinement and debate. Every generation of players has added something, questioned something, or discovered different ways of solving the same problems within the restrictions of what their bodies were reasonably able to do.

Most of us begin our musical lives knowing almost nothing about how our bodies work. So we quite rightly trust our teacher. If they say, "Hold the bow like this," or "Put your fingers here," they aren't really trying to teach a position. They are trying to lead us towards an experience. Unfortunately, experiences are very difficult to describe in words. So the teacher does the next best thing. They show us where to put our fingers and hope that, one day, we will have the experience for ourselves. It's rather like handing someone a strawberry. They can hold it, examine it and even describe it, but they won't know what it tastes like until they've eaten it.

The trouble is that words carry meaning of their own. Even the phrase "Hold the bow" suggests gripping it. But the bow isn't there to be held. It's there to make the string vibrate. I tell my students that the most important point of contact isn't in the hand at all. It's where the bow hair meets the string. The hand is simply there to control that point of contact. Try explaining that to a seven-year-old. Sometimes the best you can do is smile and say, "Just put your fingers here." Then wait, sometimes for years, until the mist rises.

Beginners are the least qualified people to recognise this. They don't yet have enough experience to distinguish between "this feels unfamiliar" and "this feels uncomfortable.” Unfortunately, unfamiliarity often masquerades as discomfort. We naturally assume that anything uncomfortable 'feels' wrong when in fact it may simply be unfamiliar. Good practice helps us move beyond that first impression until the unfamiliar becomes familiar. Only then do we begin to acquire the facility that eventually gives us freedom.

Eventually, after thousands of hours of playing, some players begin to experiment and make discoveries of their own. These are what I call "secrets". Not because they are hidden, but because they cannot be communicated through language. They become knowledge only through direct experience.

Only as the student begins to understand the underlying principles can they begin to think independently. From that understanding, a coherent method gradually emerges—one that fits their own physique rather than somebody else's.

It's rather like trying to fit a round peg into a square hole. The harder you push that peg, the more it becomes distorted.

The solution isn't to become square. The key is to understand that we were all round pegs to begin with, and to discover how each of us can sit securely in that square hole.That is where real practice begins. Not in the hands. Not even in the body. Practice begins in the mind.
